# Scientific Figures And Tables

Choose the figure or table that helps the reader verify the claim with the least
ambiguity. Decoration, dense legends, and oversized panel counts are usually
signs of unresolved argument structure.

## Start With The Claim

For each artifact, define:

- the exact claim or comparison it supports
- the audience and venue constraints
- the minimum data needed to verify that claim
- the uncertainty, controls, and exclusions that must remain visible

If one figure is trying to prove three unrelated points, split it.

## Figure Rules

- Use the simplest chart that preserves the needed comparison.
- Put axes, units, sample identity, and transformation state on the figure, not
  only in the caption.
- Show uncertainty in a form consistent with the statistical summary.
- Keep scales, colors, and reference groups consistent across related panels.
- Use panel letters only when the panels form one argument.
- Move secondary sensitivity analyses or exhaustive variants to supplement.
- Do not replace a missing real experimental image with an AI-generated one.
  If a schematic is needed, label it as schematic rather than evidence.

## Table Rules

- Use tables for exact values, inclusion/exclusion details, cohort summaries,
  model coefficients, and multi-field comparison.
- Order rows and columns by reader task, not spreadsheet accident.
- Keep units and denominators visible.
- Separate descriptive tables from inferential result tables when that improves
  reading.

## Captions

A strong caption states:

1. What is shown.
2. What the groups, symbols, or colors mean.
3. What statistical summary or uncertainty is displayed.
4. Any critical preprocessing or exclusion that changes interpretation.
5. The constrained conclusion the reader may draw.

Do not hide sample removal, normalization, baseline definition, or multiple-test
scope in a supplement-only note when it changes the main interpretation.

## Presentation Boundary

For slide decks, simplify density, enlarge the visual hierarchy, and shorten the
caption into a spoken takeaway. For manuscripts, preserve enough static detail
that the figure stands without narration.

## Completion Criteria

- Each figure or table supports one primary claim.
- Labels, units, and uncertainty are visible.
- Supplementary versus main-text placement is justified.
- Captions and body text make the same claim.
