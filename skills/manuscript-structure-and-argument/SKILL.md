# Manuscript Structure And Argument

Write the paper as a sequence of decisions: what question is answered, why the
reader should care, what evidence supports it, and what limitation remains.

## Inputs

Require:

- target artifact: paper, short communication, thesis chapter, abstract,
  response letter, or rebuttal
- target venue or house style if one exists
- central research question, answer, and claimed contribution
- available evidence and section materials
- length limit, figure/table budget, and deadline state

If the contribution or audience is still unclear, define them before polishing
sentences.

## Section Contracts

Use these purposes explicitly:

- **Title**: topic, object, and differentiating claim without overselling.
- **Abstract**: question, approach, principal evidence, bounded result, and
  significance.
- **Introduction**: established context, gap, question, and contribution.
- **Methods**: inputs, design, software, parameters, and exclusions needed to
  understand scope.
- **Results**: observation first, evidence second, interpretation separated.
- **Discussion**: meaning, comparison to prior work, competing explanations, and
  limitations.
- **Conclusion**: supported answer and bounded next step.
- **Rebuttal**: reviewer concern, exact manuscript change, and unresolved limit.

## Revision Moves

When improving a draft, choose one dominant move at a time:

1. **Restructure**: reorder paragraphs or sections so each opens with its role.
2. **Compress**: remove repeated motivation, duplicated results, and generic
   scene-setting.
3. **Clarify**: replace broad claims with the exact comparison, unit, or scope.
4. **Align**: make title, abstract, figures, and conclusion tell the same story.
5. **Adapt**: fit the venue's article type, section order, and reader knowledge.

Do not repair a weak argument by adding adjectives, hype, or vague novelty
claims.

## Paragraph Rules

- Open with the paragraph's purpose, not a generic topic sentence.
- Keep one claim-evidence move per paragraph when possible.
- Move mechanical detail to methods, supplement, or caption when it interrupts
  the argument.
- End with the implication or transition that justifies the next paragraph.

## Common Failure Modes

- The abstract claims more than the results section demonstrates.
- The introduction motivates a different question than the one analyzed.
- Results mix observation, interpretation, and speculation in one sentence.
- The discussion repeats the results instead of interpreting their scope.
- The rebuttal answers a stronger or weaker reviewer claim than was asked.

## Completion Criteria

- Every section has a defined role and no major role is missing.
- The central claim appears consistently across title, abstract, results, and
  conclusion.
- The draft fits the venue length and artifact budget.
- Open questions, weak spots, and material gaps are named explicitly.
